What you need to do, in priority order:
Replace the local freshness artifact with a real public-benchmark evaluation.
Right now the paper’s most vulnerable point is that the freshness story is not a public benchmark run. A journal reviewer will immediately attack that. At minimum, you should run on FreshQA itself, not just a local snapshot inspired by it, and scale the update-heavy evaluation far beyond 15 episodes. Your own draft says this is future work; for a journal submission, it needs to become completed work. FreshQA was introduced precisely to test up-to-date knowledge behavior, so using it directly is the cleanest fix.
Evaluate on multiple backbones, not just one small model.
A journal reviewer will not be satisfied with “the controller works on flan-t5-small.” You need at least a small + mid-size comparison, and ideally two different model families, so you can show the routing idea is not a quirk of one backbone. Your draft explicitly says it does not establish cross-model robustness.
Run against the closest prior-art baselines, not only simplified baselines.
Your current comparisons are useful, but a journal paper needs stronger neighbors. The obvious families are: selective retrieval systems such as Self-RAG, explicit read-write memory systems such as MemLLM, and lifelong/model-editing systems such as WISE and MELO. This field is already crowded, so the paper has to win its space by showing exactly where unified multi-timescale routing beats or complements these alternatives.
Evaluate lifelong behavior the way the editing literature now expects.
Journal reviewers in this area will want more than task accuracy and stale-answer rate. The model-editing literature now routinely cares about reliability, generalization, locality, portability, and especially sequential-edit forgetting. Recent work shows that editing systems can suffer both gradual and catastrophic forgetting at scale, so your “guarded consolidation + rollback” story needs stress tests over long edit sequences, not just a few hand-designed episodes.
Scale the PopQA side beyond the current slice.
PopQA is a 14k-question benchmark, and your paper currently uses a 500-example recurring-heavy slice. That is acceptable for an artifact-backed preprint, but too narrow for a journal claim unless you justify it as a focused study and then add a broader run. The journal-safe version is: evaluate on the focused recurring slice and on a much larger subset or full PopQA, then show where the gains appear and where they do not.
Make the paper’s main claim sharper and more falsifiable.
Right now the paper says unified routing is better than optimizing memory operations in isolation. That is good, but a journal paper should tighten this into a claim like: “Under recurring-query and update-heavy regimes, unified routing yields a better quality/latency/write-cost frontier than any single-family policy.” Then prove it empirically with Pareto curves, regime sweeps, and failure-mode analysis. JMLR explicitly wants papers to show what was learned, not merely what was built, and TMLR explicitly says claims must match the evidence.
Add statistical discipline and robustness checks.
Use multiple seeds, confidence intervals, significance testing where appropriate, and sensitivity analyses for your reward weights, routing masks, recurrence estimator, and consolidation thresholds. A journal reviewer will ask whether the gains survive small changes to the controller. This matters especially because your current empirical story is narrow and partly policy-driven. The general journal bar in ML/AI is clear: claims should be supported by convincing evidence, and experiments should be reproducible.
Release a full reproducibility package, not just frozen bundles.
You are already in a good place here because the draft mentions frozen traces, manifests, ablations, manual audits, and exact regeneration commands. For journal submission, go further: release code, configs, prompts, benchmark construction scripts, raw traces, evaluation scripts, and documentation. TMLR encourages reproducibility materials, JAIR now uses a reproducibility checklist, TACL allows appendices for replication details, and Nature-style AI journals require data/code/materials availability unless restrictions are disclosed.
Keep the claims narrower than the ambition.
A good journal version does not need to claim “we solve knowledge updating.” It only needs to show that routing across memory timescales is a useful and previously under-evaluated control problem. TMLR’s policy is explicit that unsupported bold claims are a rejection risk, and that reducing claims to match the evidence is a valid way to fix a paper.
Add an ethics/limitations section that feels real, not ceremonial.
For this topic, reviewers may ask about stale evidence, bad consolidation, rollback failure, privacy of stored memories, and operational harm if such routing were used in production. TACL explicitly asks authors to articulate impact, limitations, and ethical issues, and journals such as Elsevier’s Artificial Intelligence also require transparency around AI-tool use in manuscript preparation.
