We study a controller problem for language-model systems that can answer from parameters, retrieve external evidence, read and write reusable memory, apply temporary task-time updates, or consolidate stable updates more durably. The question is not whether a unified multi-timescale router can win everywhere, but where delayed utility actually helps. We evaluate a six-action router on frozen official runs covering stable recurring memory, public changing-answer evaluation, edit propagation, and broad editing. On official PopQA recurring-memory, the router matches retrieval quality at 0.290 while reducing retrieval calls from 100.0 to 72.2; removing delayed utility drops quality to 0.090. On official FreshQA public main and sequence, retrieval is perfect across all four models, while \texttt{router\_no\_v\_future} remains near ceiling at 0.982--0.996 and soft calibration only partially repairs the full router. On official MQuAKE, retrieval reaches 0.551 while the router and \texttt{router\_no\_v\_future} reach only 0.276--0.284 and 0.319--0.338. On official UniEdit, all modes are near ceiling at 0.998--1.000 and therefore largely nondiagnostic. These results support a simple empirical law: delayed utility helps when recurrence is stable and support can be reused safely, but retrieval-dominant behavior is more reliable in volatile public-answer and propagation-heavy regimes.
